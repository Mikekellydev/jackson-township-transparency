How to Run for Township Office

Jackson Township · DeKalb County, Indiana

This page is a general guide, based on Indiana township election practices. It is informational only and does not replace legal advice or official instructions from the DeKalb County Clerk or the Indiana Election Division.

1. Offices You Can Run For

Jackson Township has two elected township offices at the local level:

  • Township Trustee Oversees the township budget, township assistance, contracts, and day to day operations.
  • Township Advisory Board Three member board that reviews and approves the budget, tax levies, and certain contracts and serves as the legislative body of the township.

Both offices are elected to four year terms. They appear on the ballot in the general election in midterm years such as 2018, 2022, and 2026.

2. Basic Eligibility

General expectations to run for township office in Indiana include:

  • United States citizenship
  • Residence in Jackson Township
  • Voter registration at your township address
  • Willingness to run as a candidate of a political party or as an independent

The most up to date eligibility details and any special requirements should always be confirmed with the DeKalb County Clerk and the Indiana Election Division.

3. Paths to Get on the Ballot

In practice, there are three main ways a person can take office as trustee or as a member of the township advisory board.

3.1 Run in the Primary Election

This is the most common path. You choose a party, usually Republican or Democrat, and file a declaration of candidacy with the DeKalb County Clerk during the candidate filing window before the primary election. If you win the primary, your name appears on the general election ballot in November.

3.2 Run as an Independent or Minor Party Candidate

Independent or minor party candidates typically file a different set of forms and must collect signatures from a required number of voters. If the paperwork is accepted, the candidate appears on the general election ballot without running in a primary.

3.3 Fill a Vacancy by Party Caucus

If the trustee or a board member resigns or a seat becomes vacant before the end of a term, the political party that last held the seat may hold a caucus. Precinct committee members of that party meet and vote to select a replacement. The person chosen then serves out the remainder of the term.

4. Practical Steps for Jackson Township Residents

If you live in Jackson Township and are seriously considering a run for township trustee or advisory board, these are practical steps to get started.

  1. Contact the DeKalb County Clerk Ask for current information on:
    • Filing windows and deadlines for township offices
    • Required forms for party and independent candidates
    • Any campaign finance reporting requirements
  2. Decide whether you will run in a party primary or as an independent In many Indiana townships, most candidates file in a party primary. Independent runs are possible but often require more signature work.
  3. Attend township meetings Regularly attend township board meetings. Listen to how money is budgeted and how decisions are made. This helps you learn and also introduces you to the community as someone who is engaged.
  4. Clarify your message Be ready to explain why you want to serve, how you view stewardship of township funds, and how you will communicate with residents and neighborhoods such as HOAs.
  5. Build a small support team Even local campaigns benefit from a few trusted people who will help with paperwork, conversations with neighbors, and encouragement.

5. What Township Officials Actually Do

Before running for office, it is important to understand the practical responsibilities.

  • Trustee
    • Prepares the township budget and submits it for approval
    • Administers township assistance and related social support programs where applicable
    • Oversees township property and certain contracts such as fire protection agreements
    • Maintains required records and reports for state and county authorities
  • Advisory Board
    • Reviews and approves the annual budget and tax rates
    • Advises the trustee on financial and policy matters
    • Acts as the township legislative body for certain decisions set by state law

Both roles are part time and are meant to serve neighbors, not replace a full time job. Pay is set through the township budget and is reported in the same Indiana Gateway data that powers the transparency charts on the main page of this site.

6. Staying Nonpartisan and Neighbor Focused

This guide is intended for anyone who wants to serve the township with integrity, regardless of party. Healthy township government needs people who:

  • Use tax dollars carefully and transparently
  • Listen to residents and neighborhoods
  • Are willing to ask hard questions respectfully
  • See these roles as service to neighbors, not a personal platform
